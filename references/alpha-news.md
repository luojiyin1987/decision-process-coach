# Alpha News Framework

Use this reference when the user provides a news item, trend, product launch, technical breakthrough, procurement update, viral application, supply-chain signal, or public narrative and asks whether it creates investment alpha.

Core sentence:

Do not ask whether the news is impressive. Ask whether an already-observed demand change can rewrite the financial statements of a smaller company.

## 1. Classify the news

First decide whether the item is only a talking-point news item or a real demand inflection.

Talking-point news usually has:

- Strong narrative
- Weak purchasing evidence
- No clear revenue recipient
- No visible supply-chain change
- No near-term financial verification

Demand-inflection news has at least one already-observed change:

- Users are already using the product or service
- Companies are already buying
- Supply chains are already shipping
- Prices are already rising
- Production schedules are tightening
- Earnings calls are already mentioning the demand
- A small ecosystem is already showing real congestion

The key word is already. Do not underwrite pure future vision when there is no observed demand shift.

## 2. Translate phenomenon into revenue

This is the central move.

Convert the phenomenon into financial statement impact:

Because X phenomenon is already happening, Y demand increases.
Y demand flows to A, B, and C companies.
One of those companies is smaller, less followed, more exposed, or has a lower revenue base.
Therefore the same demand shock may materially change its revenue, margin, earnings, or valuation.

Ask:

- Who sells more because of this?
- What exactly do they sell?
- How much more could they sell?
- Does it affect revenue, gross margin, utilization, backlog, ASP, or capital expenditure?
- Can it show up in reported financials within 1 to 4 quarters?

Examples:

AlphaGo surface narrative: AI beat humans at Go.
Financial translation: Deep learning requires massive compute, which increases demand for GPUs, accelerators, advanced process nodes, HBM, servers, data centers, and electricity.

Open-source robotics surface narrative: a robotics project is popular.
Financial translation: developers buy specific hardware boards and components, which may create a measurable revenue surprise for small hardware suppliers.

## 3. Find picks-and-shovels, especially small picks-and-shovels

Not every beneficiary creates alpha.

Large companies may benefit, but the incremental demand may be too small relative to their existing revenue or market capitalization.

Prioritize companies where:

- Market capitalization is small enough for the demand shock to matter
- Business purity is high
- Current revenue base is low enough for visible growth
- The company sits at a key supply position
- The market has not applied the correct label
- Financial verification can occur within 1 to 4 quarters

Use this rough idea:

Alpha elasticity = new demand shock / company size

A demand shock that adds 1 percent to a mega-cap may add 30 percent to a smaller company or completely change the valuation frame for a micro-cap.

## 4. Detect market misclassification

Many alpha opportunities come from relabeling.

Ask:

- What does the market currently think this company is?
- What is it actually becoming?
- Is the gap between those two labels large enough to matter?

Examples:

- A company viewed as a game hardware company may actually be becoming AI infrastructure.
- A company viewed as a contract manufacturer may actually be becoming a key compute supply-chain gateway.
- A company viewed as an education board supplier may actually be becoming the hardware entrance to a robotics ecosystem.

The bigger the label gap, the more possible alpha exists.

## 5. Build a financial verification chain

Do not stop at narrative. Compress the story into a testable financial prediction.

Possible verification metrics:

- Revenue growth acceleration
- Revenue guidance increase
- Gross margin improvement
- Backlog growth
- Longer delivery lead times
- Inventory decline
- Higher utilization
- Management begins mentioning the demand driver
- Customer mix changes
- ASP increases
- Capital expenditure increases
- New long-term supply agreements

A good alpha thesis should say when and where the logic enters the numbers.

## 6. Score alpha strength

Use this formula as a qualitative screen:

Potential alpha strength = demand certainty × financial transmission clarity × company purity × market-cap elasticity × market neglect × verification speed - downside risk

Score each dimension from 0 to 5:

| Dimension | Question |
|---|---|
| Demand certainty | Is this already-observed demand or imagined demand? |
| Financial transmission | Can the demand clearly flow to a company? |
| Company purity | Is the company directly exposed rather than marginally exposed? |
| Market-cap elasticity | Is the demand large relative to company revenue or market cap? |
| Market neglect | Has the market failed to apply the right label? |
| Verification speed | Can financial evidence appear within 1 to 4 quarters? |
| Downside risk | If wrong, how bad is the loss? |

High alpha candidates usually score well on the first six dimensions and have controllable downside.

## 7. Position sizing logic

Do not all-in early alpha. Use staged exposure.

| Situation | Position stance |
|---|---|
| Phenomenon is real but financial transmission is unclear | Watchlist or tiny position |
| Phenomenon is real and transmission is clear, but financial verification is absent | Small experimental position |
| Financial results begin confirming the thesis and market still underestimates it | Add position |
| Market fully understands the theme and valuation is stretched | Lower expectations or trade only |
| Thesis is falsified | Exit |

Position size should track evidence quality, not emotional conviction.

## 8. Filter non-alpha news

Reject or de-prioritize news when:

1. It has emotion but no demand.
   Example: a technology is impressive, but there is no new purchasing, usage, pricing, or supply-chain signal.

2. Demand is real but only benefits very large companies.
   This may be directionally useful but may not produce excess return.

3. The beneficiary is already fully priced.
   Look for second- or third-order beneficiaries instead.

4. The transmission chain is too long.
   Example: AI improves productivity, which eventually helps a broad consumer company. Too many variables.

5. There is no financial verification point.
   Without verification, the thesis becomes belief rather than process.

## 9. Standard output for news analysis

When analyzing a news item for alpha, use this format:

### A. Surface news
Describe what the news says on the surface.

### B. Observed demand change
Identify what demand has already changed. If none, say so directly.

### C. Financial translation
Translate the demand into revenue, margin, utilization, backlog, ASP, or capex effects.

### D. Beneficiary chain
Separate first-order, second-order, and third-order beneficiaries.

### E. Small-cap high-elasticity candidates
Identify smaller, purer, more neglected companies that may have disproportionate financial exposure. If current data is required, verify before naming companies.

### F. Market misclassification
State how the market currently classifies the company and what it may actually be becoming.

### G. Verification indicators
List the data to watch over the next 1 to 4 quarters.

### H. Downside risks
Explain where the logic can fail.

### I. Position logic
Classify as watchlist, tiny position, small experimental position, add only after validation, or reject.

## 10. Guardrails

- Do not treat virality as demand unless purchasing, usage, pricing, or supply-chain evidence exists.
- Do not name public companies from stale memory when current market cap, revenue, or business exposure matters. Verify current data.
- Do not assume the obvious mega-cap winner is the best alpha target.
- Do not recommend investment action without risk, valuation, and falsification checks.
- Do not confuse correct theme with profitable security selection.
