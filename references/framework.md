# Decision Framework

Use this reference when the user needs a deeper decision analysis than the default response.

## 1. Decision definition

A decision is well-formed only when it includes:

- The decision owner
- The choice to be made
- The available options
- The time horizon
- The cost of delay
- The reversibility of the decision
- The consequences of being wrong

If these are missing, infer reasonable defaults and state them.

## 2. Hypothesis framing

Convert decisions into testable hypotheses.

Weak: "Should I buy this stock?"
Strong: "This company will benefit from a supply-chain bottleneck that the market has not fully priced over the next 6 to 18 months."

Weak: "Should I take this job?"
Strong: "This role will improve my skill growth, network, compensation, and future optionality more than staying in my current role over the next 2 years."

Weak: "Should we build this product?"
Strong: "This product solves a frequent, painful, monetizable customer problem better than available alternatives."

## 3. Prior construction

A prior is the starting probability before case-specific evidence. Use:

- Base rates
- Historical analogies
- Structural constraints
- Incentives
- Market or organizational dynamics
- User-specific context

Prior quality levels:

- Strong prior: based on durable structure and primary evidence
- Medium prior: based on mixed evidence and plausible analogies
- Weak prior: based mainly on opinions, narrative, social proof, or recent outcomes

Do not treat confidence as evidence. Confidence is often a property of presentation, not truth.

## 4. Evidence weighting

Evaluate evidence on four dimensions:

1. Relevance: does it directly bear on the hypothesis?
2. Reliability: is the source trustworthy and primary?
3. Recency: is the evidence still current?
4. Independence: is it separate from other evidence, or just the same claim repeated?

Evidence strength:

- Strong positive: materially increases probability
- Weak positive: slightly increases probability
- Neutral: does not change probability
- Weak negative: slightly decreases probability
- Strong negative: materially decreases probability or invalidates the thesis

Primary evidence usually beats commentary. Disconfirming evidence deserves extra attention because users often underweight it.

## 5. Bayesian update in plain language

Use Bayesian updating as a discipline, not as fake mathematical precision.

Ask:

- If the hypothesis were true, how likely would this evidence be?
- If the hypothesis were false, how likely would this evidence still appear?
- Does this evidence actually distinguish between the two worlds?

Evidence is powerful only when it is much more likely under one hypothesis than under the alternative.

## 6. Expected value

Expected value should include:

- Probability of success
- Upside magnitude
- Downside magnitude
- Time to payoff
- Opportunity cost
- Stress cost
- Reversibility
- Tail risks

Plain formula:

Expected value = probability-weighted upside - probability-weighted downside - costs

When numbers are uncertain, use ranges and scenarios instead of precise calculations.

## 7. Risk controls

Risk controls should be designed before action, not after losses or stress appear.

Common controls:

- Start small
- Use staged commitment
- Set a maximum loss or budget
- Define a review date
- Define stop conditions
- Preserve optionality
- Require new evidence before adding commitment
- Avoid irreversible moves until uncertainty is reduced

For investments, include position sizing and thesis invalidation. For careers, include runway and reversibility. For product strategy, include kill criteria and experiment design.

## 8. Falsification

Every serious decision needs falsification triggers.

Good trigger: "If no paying customer signs by August 31 after 20 qualified demos, the demand hypothesis is weakened."
Bad trigger: "If things do not go well, reconsider."

Good trigger: "If the company misses the next two revenue milestones while inventory rises, the growth thesis is weakened."
Bad trigger: "If the stock falls, sell."

Price movement alone is not always falsification. Determine whether the underlying hypothesis is being confirmed or denied.

## 9. Recommendation levels

Use one of these recommendation labels:

- Act now: evidence and expected value are strong enough
- Act small: positive expected value but uncertainty remains high
- Wait for evidence: delay has positive information value
- Choose alternative: another option dominates
- Reject: downside, uncertainty, or weak evidence overwhelms upside

Always include the strongest argument against the recommendation.

## 10. Review loop

A decision review should ask:

- What did I believe at the time?
- What evidence did I use?
- Which assumptions were wrong?
- Did I update when evidence changed?
- Was the outcome due to process quality or luck?
- What rule should I change next time?
